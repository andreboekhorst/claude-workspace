# Edit

Take existing content and improve it — tighten language, fix structure, and align tone with purpose.

## Typical flow

1. **Read** the original content in full before making any changes.
2. **Diagnose** issues — identify problems with clarity, flow, tone, redundancy, or structure.
3. **Revise** the content — apply edits while preserving the author's voice and intent.
4. **Present** the improved version alongside a summary of what changed and why.
5. **Iterate** if the user wants further adjustments to specific sections.

## Inputs

- The draft or content to be edited.
- The purpose and intended audience of the piece.
- Specific concerns the user has (e.g., "too long", "too formal", "unclear intro").
- Style or tone guidelines (optional).

## Outputs

- An improved version of the content.
- A brief change summary noting the key edits made.

## Quality signals

- Every edit has a reason — nothing changed arbitrarily.
- The author's voice and intent are preserved, not overwritten.
- The piece reads more clearly and concisely than the original.
- Structure improvements make the content easier to scan and follow.
- The change summary is honest about what was altered.

## Common variations

- **Light pass** — fix only grammar, typos, and awkward phrasing. Leave structure alone.
- **Heavy restructure** — reorganize sections, cut significantly, rewrite weak passages.
- **Tone shift** — keep the content but adjust voice (e.g., formal to conversational).
- **Feedback only** — diagnose issues and suggest changes without rewriting.
