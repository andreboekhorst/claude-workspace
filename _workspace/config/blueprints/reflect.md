# Reflect

Guide the user through a structured reflection to capture lessons learned and turn them into actionable improvements.

## Typical flow

1. **Recap** what happened — summarize the project, event, or period under review.
2. **Identify** what worked — highlight successes, strengths, and positive surprises.
3. **Identify** what didn't — surface problems, friction, and unmet expectations.
4. **Extract** actionable lessons — turn observations into specific changes to make.
5. **Document** the reflection — compile findings into a shareable format.

## Inputs

- The subject of reflection: a project, sprint, event, habit, or time period.
- Any artifacts: notes, outcomes, metrics, feedback from others.
- The user's initial impressions (what felt good, what felt off).

## Outputs

- A reflection document containing:
  - Brief summary of what was reviewed.
  - What worked well (2-5 items).
  - What didn't work or could improve (2-5 items).
  - Lessons learned as actionable statements.
  - Specific next steps or changes to adopt.

## Quality signals

- Lessons are specific, not generic ("schedule buffer days" not "plan better").
- Both successes and failures are acknowledged honestly.
- Action items are concrete and owned — not vague intentions.
- The reflection is short enough to re-read later.
- Patterns across multiple reflections start to emerge.

## Common variations

- **Retrospective**: Team-oriented, often uses Start/Stop/Continue format.
- **Post-mortem**: Focused on a failure or incident with root cause analysis.
- **Personal journal**: Lighter, more freeform, focused on growth and feelings.
- **After-action review**: Structured military-style: what was expected, what happened, why, what to do next.
