# Critique

Deliver structured, constructive feedback that helps improve a piece of work.

## Typical flow

1. **Review** the work thoroughly — read or examine it in full before forming judgments
2. **Identify** strengths — what is working well and why
3. **Identify** areas for improvement — what could be stronger and why
4. **Prioritize** feedback — separate critical issues from nice-to-haves
5. **Frame** each point constructively — pair problems with specific suggestions
6. **Organize** feedback into a clear structure the recipient can act on
7. **Summarize** the overall assessment and recommended next steps

## Inputs

- The work to critique (document, design, code, plan, writing, etc.)
- The creator's goals or intent for the work
- Optional: specific areas where feedback is most wanted
- Optional: context about the stage of work (draft, near-final, etc.)
- Optional: who the work is for (audience, stakeholders)

## Outputs

- Feedback document with specific, actionable points organized by priority
- Strengths section highlighting what to preserve
- Improvement section with concrete suggestions, not just problems
- Overall summary with a clear recommendation (ready, needs revision, rethink)

## Quality signals

- Every critique point is specific — references exact parts of the work, not vague impressions
- Suggestions are actionable — the creator knows what to do next, not just what is wrong
- Feedback is balanced — strengths are genuine, not filler before the real critique
- Priority is clear — the creator knows what matters most to fix first
- Tone respects the work — direct and honest without being dismissive

## Common variations

- **Peer review**: Balanced, collaborative tone, emphasis on dialogue
- **Editorial feedback**: Focus on clarity, structure, and audience fit
- **Code review**: Technical accuracy, maintainability, patterns and anti-patterns
- **Design critique**: Usability, consistency, alignment with goals
- **Self-critique**: Structured reflection using the same framework on your own work
