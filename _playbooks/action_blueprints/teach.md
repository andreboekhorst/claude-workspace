# Teach

Design a complete teaching session with clear objectives, engaging activities, and built-in assessment.

## Typical flow

1. **Define** learning objectives — what should participants know or be able to do afterward?
2. **Assess** the starting point — what do participants already know, and what gaps exist?
3. **Design** activities that move participants from current knowledge to objectives
4. **Build** assessment checks — how will you know learning happened?
5. **Sequence** everything into a timed session plan with transitions
6. **Prepare** supporting materials — slides, handouts, prompts, examples
7. **Anticipate** where participants are likely to get stuck and plan interventions

## Inputs

- The subject or skill to teach
- Target audience (group size, background, experience level)
- Available time and format (in-person, remote, async)
- Optional: constraints (tools available, prerequisites, accessibility needs)
- Optional: desired outcome beyond knowledge (behavior change, certification, etc.)

## Outputs

- Session plan with objectives, activities, timing, and materials list
- Assessment checks mapped to each learning objective
- Facilitator notes with transition cues and contingency plans
- Optional: participant handouts or worksheets

## Quality signals

- Every activity directly supports at least one learning objective
- Assessment checks are specific and observable — not just "did they understand?"
- Timing is realistic with buffer for questions and slow spots
- The session has a clear arc: hook, build, practice, reflect
- Materials are ready to use, not placeholders

## Common variations

- **Workshop**: Hands-on, participant-driven, longer format with breakout activities
- **Lecture**: Information-dense, shorter interaction, emphasis on clarity and pacing
- **Training**: Skill-focused, heavy on practice and repetition, measurable outcomes
- **Onboarding**: Context-heavy, self-paced elements, reference materials for later
- **Micro-lesson**: 5-15 minutes, single concept, minimal materials
